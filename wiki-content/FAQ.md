# Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the book, the methodology, and the AI experiment.

## About the Book

### What is this book about?

"The Shadow of Extremism" analyzes how extremist movements emerge across different religions and ideologies. It identifies **5 universal dynamics** that appear whether we're looking at ISIS, the Spanish Inquisition, Buddhist nationalism, or other movements.

The core argument: **Extremism isn't about "bad religions"—it's about predictable human responses to trauma and uncertainty.**

### Who is this book for?

- **General Readers**: Curious about extremism but intimidated by academic texts
- **Students**: Looking for accessible introduction to comparative religious extremism
- **Researchers**: Interested in AI-assisted research methodologies
- **AI Enthusiasts**: Want to see what modern LLMs can produce
- **Skeptics**: Want to evaluate AI-generated content critically

### How long is it?

**200+ pages** covering:
- Historical context (Crusades, colonialism, modern jihadism)
- Theological analysis (Islamic jurisprudence, scriptural interpretation)
- Comparative cases (Buddhism, Christianity, Hinduism, Islam)
- Social science frameworks (Social Identity Theory, Moral Foundations)
- Solutions (theological reform, de-radicalization programs)

### Is it free?

**Yes.** Available free online at: https://algiras.github.io/the-shadow-of-extremism/

Also available as free PDF/EPUB downloads from GitHub Releases.

## About the Content

### Is Islam violent?

**The book's answer**: No religion is inherently violent or peaceful. **Extremism is a human problem, not a religious one.**

The same dynamics that produce ISIS also produced:
- Christian Inquisition
- Buddhist ethnic cleansing (Myanmar)
- Hindu mob violence (India)
- Secular revolutionary terror (Khmer Rouge)

When the 5 Dynamics converge, **any ideology can be weaponized**.

### What are the 5 Dynamics?

1. **The Myth of Purity**: Idealized past as template for restoration
2. **The Trauma & The Void**: Destabilization creating demand for meaning
3. **The Absolute Narrative**: Binary good-vs-evil explanation
4. **The Mechanism of Belonging**: Radicalization process binding individuals
5. **The Entropy of Violence**: Purity spirals → self-destruction

See **[[The 5 Dynamics]]** for deep dive.

### Does the book defend ISIS/extremism?

**Absolutely not.**

**The book argues:**
- Understanding ≠ Justifying
- Explaining how extremism works ≠ Excusing it
- Recognizing predictable patterns ≠ Removing moral responsibility

**Quote from the introduction:**
> "To understand is not to forgive. To explain is not to excuse. But to fight an enemy you refuse to understand is to guarantee defeat."

### What about solutions?

## Technical Questions

### How can I replicate this?

See **[[Replication Guide]]** for complete step-by-step instructions.

**TL;DR**:
1. Pick comparative topic
2. Use free AI tools (Antigravity, Claude)
3. Prompt strategically (be specific, demand citations, iterate)
4. Verify relentlessly (cross-check, fact-check, remove hallucinations)
5. Curate ruthlessly (keep good, discard bad, edit everything)
6. Be transparent (acknowledge AI involvement and limitations)

### What were the biggest challenges?

**Top 5:**

1. **Hallucinations**: AI invents plausible-sounding sources that don't exist
2. **Verification time**: Fact-checking is slower than generation
3. **Theological nuance**: AI sometimes oversimplifies complex debates
4. **Citation format**: AI inconsistent with academic citation styles
5. **Knowing when to stop**: AI can generate infinitely; deciding "enough" is hard

### What would you do differently?

**Improvements for v2:**

- **More verification time**: Allocate 3-4 days instead of 2
- **Better citation workflow**: Create systematic tracking from start
- **Expert review**: Get actual scholars to review pre-publication
- **Multi-stage prompting**: Separate research → outline → draft → refine more clearly
- **Version control per chapter**: Track AI outputs vs human edits better

### Will AI replace researchers?

**No, but it will transform how research happens.**

**AI is excellent at:**
- Processing large datasets quickly
- Identifying patterns across sources
- Generating initial drafts
- Multi-language analysis

**Humans are still essential for:**
- Asking the right questions
- Judging source quality
- Contextualizing findings
- Ethical decision-making
- Taking responsibility

**The future**: Not "AI alone" or "human alone" but **human-AI collaboration**.
